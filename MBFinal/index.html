What is a Tragic Hero?

A tragic hero is a character of high stature who suffers a downfall because of their own choices and flaws.

1. Nobility or High Stature

Macbeth begins as a respected warrior and noble, admired by society. He is trusted by King Duncan and holds power as Thane of Glamis.

Example: Society looks up to Macbeth, more specifically King Duncan looks up to Macbeth.

2. Hamartia (Tragic Flaw)

Macbeth’s ambition drives him to pursue power at any cost. This flaw leads him to make destructive choices.

Example: Macbeth kills King Duncan after being told his prophecy as king.

3. Peripeteia (Reversal of Fortune)

Macbeth’s success turns to disaster. His plans unravel, power slips away, and events spiral out of control.

Example: After Duncan's death, Macbeth is stuck in a cycle of killing, guilt, and death.

4. Anagnorisis (Moment of Realization)

Macbeth realizes his ambition has led to ruin. He sees the truth about himself and the consequences of his actions.

Example: Macbeth didn't see that the witches gave him a double-sided philosophy when Macduff was ripped from his mother's womb untimely.

5. Catastrophe (The Fall)

Macbeth faces complete downfall—he loses power, relationships, and ultimately his life. Justice is restored.

Example: Macbeth’s death marks the end of his journey. Malcolm takes his righteous place as king.

6. Catharsis (Audience’s Emotional Response)

The audience feels pity for Macbeth’s lost greatness and fear because his flaws are deeply human.

Example: The audience feels pity because Macbeth had what he was granted and went insane. We feel fear because anyone can have this flaw.
